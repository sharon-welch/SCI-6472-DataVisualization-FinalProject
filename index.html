Are your customers comfortable on their bikes?

An argument for a new saddle design for women


Cycling is associated with a host of chronic health problems.
These issues might be exacerbated in women — increasing their discomfort,
decreasing their ridership, and ultimately hurting your bottom line.

But what is it that is negatively affecting female cyclists?

In the US, only 2.2% of trips are taken by bike. Furthermore, only .6% are taken by women.
Reasons for large existing gender disparities in biking are not fully understood.

In looking at national data on daily commutes, only 27% of trips are made by women.

Furthermore, women's health and comfort when it comes to bicycling are understudied. For example, saddle sores are an issue often reported by cyclists. A review of literature conducted in 2022 showed that only 2/15 of the most prevalent studies on saddle sores focused on women.


Each pie segment represents a single study


One theory is that the comfort of the bike, and especially the saddle, influence ridership

While existing research has shown that women feel less safe while biking and are generally less willing to bike in areas without cycling infrastructure, there has been very little research done to explore whether design of the bike itself influences female ridership.

Here, we explore existing factors that influence health of cyclists to search for potential correlations between health, bike seat design, and ridership disparities. This exploration is based on the hunch that saddle design does in fact influence ridership.

Although there are fewer female cyclists, comparable numbers of men and women experience many of these symptoms (i.e. the rate is higher in women). Further, women tend to be more affected than men even when having less intense cycling habits (lower length of time cycling, less frequent, shorter length, slower rides, etc.).

Of the predictors that affect these health outcomes, a handful can be modified by cyclists, without affecting the intensity or frequency of their workouts. One such factor — the type of saddle — seems like it might be particularly influential.


In particular, saddle type interacts with the body of the rider, in a way that differs between males and females. We examine how pressure differs by saddle type (stratified by gender) and how that related to reported health outcomes.

Health Outcomes

As you click through the different Health Outcomes, you will observe that although the rate of these issues changes as a function of saddle type, a higher rate of many of these issues in female cyclists. An interesting exception to this is the Complete Cutout Saddle, reinforcing the fact that the saddle may be one of the most effective ways to improve health outcomes in female cyclists.


Scientific research shows that women have worse health outcomes following cycling, which in part is an effect of pressure distribution resulting from saddle design. Moving forward, it is critical to take into account pressure distribution in saddle design.

Saddle design is an essential component of comfort for cyclists.

While these issues affect both men and women, women appear to be disproportionately affected.


In spite of the high rates of chronic health outcomes in cyclists, these issues are understudied across the board.
The data that does exist suggests that these issues impact women at a much higher rate.

There is good news — the data that exists suggests type of saddle may mediate these outcomes, and that it may have
this effect through differential pressure patterns between men and women.

Further research into these questions lead to innovations in saddle design, not only improving comfort for female cyclists,
it also has the power to increase the number of women that cycle and, ultimately, the number of loyal customers you have.
